utopia, distopia, aetopia

research update: 09/25/2025

When building a sandbox for collaborative storytelling, we wanted to go beyond individual characters and plots. We wanted to create a framework where players could experiment with worlds; with societies, ideologies, and environments that shape the lives within them. That is why utopia and dystopia are at the heart of our aesthetic vision.

In our sandbox, players can construct their own utopian experiments. These worlds serve both as aspirational blueprints and as provocations. What happens when ideals meet human complexity? Sir Thomas More’s Utopia offered one of the earliest thought experiments in designing a perfect society. His imagined island featured communal property, rational governance, and social harmony. But, it was also a satire, inviting readers to question whether perfection on paper can ever align with the complexities of human life.

Frank Lloyd Wright’s Broadacre City imagined a suburban utopia of decentralized living, where every family had land and technology connected people across distances. It was visionary but also deeply tied to American ideals of property and mobility.

The Panopticon, for example, designed by philosopher Jeremy Bentham, was a circular prison where every inmate could be watched by a single guard, an architecture of total visibility and control. Today, it serves as a metaphor for surveillance culture. In our sandbox, players might design a city whose layout enforces obedience, or test what happens when every citizen lives under the gaze of authority.

Novels like George Orwell’s 1984 and Aldous Huxley’s Brave New World showed two dystopias. 1984 imagines totalitarian control through fear, surveillance, and repression, while Brave New World depicts a society lulled into obedience by comfort, pleasure, and distraction. Together, they frame two poles of dystopia: oppression by pain, and oppression by pleasure.

Similarly, John B. Calhoun’s “mouse utopia” experiments, often called the “Behavioral Sink”, tested what happens when a population of mice lives in a space of abundance but limited structure. The result was chaos: overcrowding, social collapse, and the eventual extinction of the colony.

We don’t present utopia and dystopia as opposites, but as two sides of the same coin. Every utopia contains the seeds of dystopia, and every dystopia reflects a distorted dream of order. Could either ever trully exist? As the original etymology for these words, aetopia, decribes a place of "nowhere." In our sandbox, players are not given answers but possibilities.

However, by weaving utopian and dystopian aesthetics into our storytelling framework, we allow players to grapple with the essential question of world-building: not just what is possible, but what is desirable, and at what cost.
